3. Regulatory Architecture: The Friction of Compliance
The scaling model must navigate the complex regulatory environment of Ontario, specifically the Regulated Health Professions Act (RHPA) and the standards set by the College of Registered Psychotherapists of Ontario (CRPO). These regulations act as "brakes" on aggressive commercial strategies.

3.1 The Prohibition on Referral Fees (CRPO Standard 1.6)
A critical constraint for any "scaling" model that aggregates clients is the prohibition on referral fees. CRPO Standard 1.6 explicitly states that registrants cannot pay or accept fees for referrals.9

The Risk: If Next Step Therapy is perceived merely as a marketing engine that takes a 30% cut for "finding the client," this violates the standard. The College views this as "fee-splitting" or a kickback scheme, which compromises the therapist's professional judgment.

The Operational Requirement: To justify the revenue split (the 30% retained by the practice), the business must provide tangible, fair-market-value services to the clinicians. This includes:

Clinical Supervision: Not just administrative oversight, but clinical guidance.11

Scaling Impact: This requirement forces the business to be "heavy." You cannot simply use AI to route leads to therapists and collect a check. You must build a Clinic, with all the associated overhead, liability, and management costs. This raises the break-even point significantly.

3.2 The Independent Contractor vs. Employee Minefield
Most Ontario group practices, including the model implied by Next Step Therapy, rely on Independent Contractors to deliver care. This avoids the ~15-20% burden of employer payroll taxes (CPP, EI, EHT) and benefits. However, the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) and the CRPO create conflicting pressures here.

The Quality Control Paradox: To build a brand like "Next Step Therapy" that promises "expert" care 1, the practice owner needs to control the quality of the service. This means mandating specific modalities (e.g., ACT/CBT), requiring specific note-taking formats, and enforcing attendance policies.

The Legal Consequence: The more control the practice exerts (to ensure brand consistency), the more the relationship looks like Employment under CRA rules.12 If the CRA audits and reclassifies the 30 therapists as employees, the practice would be liable for retroactive payroll taxes and vacation pay, which could amount to hundreds of thousands of dollars, instantly rendering the business insolvent.

Scaling Impact: To scale safely as a contractor model, the practice must essentially function as a loose confederation of autonomous clinicians, which dilutes the brand. If a client has a bad experience with Contractor A, they blame "Next Step Therapy," even though Next Step Therapy (legally) cannot force Contractor A to change their clinical approach without risking reclassification.

3.3 Clinical Supervision and The "Qualifying" Trap
Scaling often tempts owners to hire Registered Psychotherapists (Qualifying) or RP-Qs. These are newer therapists who are cheaper or willing to accept lower splits (e.g., 50/50) in exchange for supervision hours.13

The Hidden Cost: While the gross margin looks better (50% vs. 30%), the operational drag is immense. RP-Qs require frequent supervision (e.g., 1 hour for every 4-5 client hours) and sign-off on notes.

Brand Erosion: Placing RP-Qs in front of "high-functioning professionals" (lawyers, executives) is risky. This demographic expects seniority. A bad clinical match due to inexperience can lead to negative Google Reviews, which are fatal for a digital-first practice.

Regulatory Burden: The practice owner (Jesse Cynamon) is professionally responsible for the clinical actions of the RP-Qs under his supervision. At $5M scale, the liability exposure of supervising 20+ junior therapists is catastrophic.
