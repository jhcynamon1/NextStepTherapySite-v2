Is This You?

You can't stop thinking about work, even when you're home. The day ends but your brain doesn't stop processing. Conversations with your boss keep replaying. Emails you need to send tomorrow. Projects that might go wrong. You're physically present with your family, but mentally you're still at work.

The uncertainty feels constant. Job security, the economy, raising kids in a world that feels unpredictable. You try to plan for everything, but there's always something else to worry about. The attempt to control it all is exhausting, and it doesn't actually work.

You've been told to "just relax" but you can't. People say not to worry so much, like that's something you haven't tried. If you could flip a switch and turn off the anxiety, you would have done it years ago. It's not that simple.

The physical symptoms are real. Tight shoulders, chest pressure, trouble sleeping, stomach issues. Your body is keeping score of all the stress, even when your mind tries to push through. You know it's connected, but knowing doesn't make it stop.

If any of this lands, you're not broken. You're dealing with a lot. And virtual therapy can meet you where you are.

How Anxiety Therapy Actually Helps

Therapy isn't about fixing you. You're not broken. It's about understanding what's driving the anxiety and developing a different relationship with it.

Here's what I've seen: the anxiety usually makes sense. There are real pressures. Real responsibilities. Real reasons to worry about job security, finances, and family. The problem isn't that you care about these things. The problem is that the worry has taken over, and you've lost the ability to step back from it.

I use Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which has strong research support. But the specific approach matters less than whether we actually connect and whether our conversations help you see things differently.

What we typically work on:

  • Understanding why your anxiety shows up when and how it does
  • Developing awareness without judgment (harder than it sounds)
  • Getting clear on what you actually value, not just what you "should" do
  • Building flexibility in how you respond to stress
  • Learning to be present instead of always three steps ahead

The goal isn't to eliminate anxiety. That's not realistic. The goal is to stop letting it run your life.
