Does This Sound Like You?

Many professionals in Toronto describe anxiety that shows up in predictable patterns. You might recognize yourself in these experiences:

Common Signs of Anxiety:

  • Sunday Night Dread: That sinking feeling as the weekend ends and Monday approaches
  • TTC Anxiety: Your heart races on crowded trains or during rush hour commutes
  • Work Performance Pressure: Constant worry about making mistakes or not being good enough
  • The 3am Mind Race: Your brain won't stop replaying every conversation from the day
  • Social Exhaustion: Networking events or after-work drinks feel like performance art
  • Physical Symptoms: Tension headaches, tight chest, or stomach issues when stressed
  • Decision Paralysis: Even simple choices feel overwhelming
  • Avoidance Patterns: Finding reasons to skip social situations or postpone important tasks

If several of these resonate with you, you're not brokenâ€”you're human. Anxiety is incredibly common, especially among driven, thoughtful people living in demanding environments like Toronto.

The good news? You don't have to accept this as your normal. Evidence-based therapy can help you develop a different relationship with anxiety.

How ACT Therapy Helps with Anxiety

Therapy for anxiety isn't about trying to convince you that your worries are irrationalâ€”often they're not. Instead, it's about developing practical skills to manage anxiety when it shows up.

We use Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which has extensive research support for anxiety disorders. Rather than trying to eliminate anxious thoughts and feelings, ACT helps you change your relationship with them so they don't control your behavior.

What This Looks Like in Practice:

Notice Without Judgment

Recognize anxiety as information rather than truth. Learn to observe anxious thoughts without automatically believing them.

Choose Your Response

Develop space between feeling anxious and acting anxiously. Anxiety can be present without controlling your decisions.

Take Valued Action

Move toward what matters to you, even when anxiety is present. Values guide you better than fears.

Build Tolerance

Increase your capacity to handle uncertainty and discomfort without immediately trying to escape it.

This isn't about positive thinking or pretending problems don't exist. It's about developing psychological flexibilityâ€”the ability to stay present with whatever you're experiencing while still making choices based on your values rather than your fears.

Many clients find that as they develop these skills, their relationship with anxiety fundamentally changes. Instead of seeing anxiety as something wrong with them, they begin to understand it as one part of their experience that doesn't have to dictate their choices.
