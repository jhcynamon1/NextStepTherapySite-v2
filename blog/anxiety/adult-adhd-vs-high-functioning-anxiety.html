Adult ADHD or High-Functioning Anxiety? Why You Can't Focus

Published: November 19, 2025 | Jesse Cynamon, Registered Psychotherapist (Qualifying)

Important Medical Disclaimer: I am a Registered Psychotherapist, not a Psychologist or Psychiatrist. I do not diagnose ADHD.

This article explores the subjective experience of focus and anxiety. It is for educational purposes only and is not a substitute for a formal medical assessment. If you suspect you have ADHD, I encourage you to speak with your family doctor or a psychologist for a diagnosis. Therapy can support the symptoms of stress and emotional regulation regardless of the diagnosis.

You’re successful on paper. You hit your targets, you show up to meetings, and you get the job done. But inside, you feel like you’re constantly scrambling.

You sit down to work, and your brain feels like a browser with 50 tabs open. You pick up your phone, check email, stare at the wall, and suddenly it’s 2 PM and you haven’t started the "big task."

Is it undiagnosed Adult ADHD? Or is it High-Functioning Anxiety?

The symptoms often look identical from the outside: procrastination, brain fog, irritability, and exhaustion. But the internal driver is often very different.

The Internal Experience: "I Can't Start" vs. "I'm Scared to Start"

In my practice with high-performing professionals in Ontario, I often see clients who are convinced they have ADHD because they can't focus. Sometimes they do. But often, what looks like an attention deficit is actually a nervous system in a state of chronic threat.

Here is how the internal monologue often differs:

The Symptom The ADHD Experience (Executive Function) The Anxiety Experience (Emotional Regulation)
Procrastination "I want to do it, but I physically can't make myself start. My brain keeps sliding off the task." "I want to do it, but I'm terrified it won't be perfect, so I'm avoiding the feeling of potential failure."
Distraction "Oh, look at that bird! I wonder what kind it is. I should Google that. Wait, what was I doing?" "What if I get fired? Did I send that email? I need to check Slack again to make sure I'm safe."
Exhaustion Drained from the effort of trying to organize a chaotic brain. Drained from the physical tension of being in "fight-or-flight" mode all day.

The "Burnout Mimic"

There is a third player in this game: Burnout.

When you have been running on high-functioning anxiety for years—white-knuckling through your career—your nervous system eventually hits a wall. This is often when clients call me saying, "I think I developed ADHD."

Chronic stress damages the prefrontal cortex—the part of your brain responsible for focus and planning. A burnt-out brain looks almost exactly like an ADHD brain. You become forgetful, scattered, and emotionally reactive.

How Therapy Helps (Without a Diagnosis)

Whether you have a neurodevelopmental condition (ADHD) or an emotional regulation issue (Anxiety), the result is the same: You are suffering, and you can't work the way you want to.

Therapy isn't about slapping a label on you; it's about calming the system.

1. We Calm the Physiology

We use techniques to down-regulate your nervous system. If your brain is in "survival mode," it cannot focus on a spreadsheet. It is scanning for tigers. By lowering your baseline stress, we often see focus return naturally.

2. The "Process of Elimination"

Think of therapy as a diagnostic experiment. If we treat the anxiety and the focus issues disappear, it was likely anxiety. If we treat the anxiety and you feel calmer but still cannot organize your thoughts, that gives us valuable data to take to a psychologist for a formal ADHD assessment.

3. Practical Skills for Regulation

We work on "unhooking" from the perfectionism and fear that drives procrastination. We build routines that support a tired brain, rather than beating it into submission.

You Don't Need a Label to Deserve Support

You don't need to wait for a formal diagnosis to start feeling better. If you are struggling to manage your workload and your wellbeing, therapy can help you untangle the knot.
