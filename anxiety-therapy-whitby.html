Is This You?

The commute eats your energy before you even start. You moved to Whitby for the space, the schools, the quality of life. But the trade-off is that GO Train ride. And somewhere on that train, your brain starts running scenarios about work, about home, about everything you need to handle. By the time you walk through the door, you're already spent.

You're holding it together, but barely. The job is demanding. The kids need things. The house needs things. You're juggling everything, and from the outside it probably looks fine. But inside there's this constant hum of anxiety that never quite goes away. Even when you're supposed to be relaxing, your mind is somewhere else.

The weekends don't feel like enough. Saturday morning comes and you're still recovering from the week. By Sunday night, you're already dreading Monday. The cycle keeps repeating, and you're wondering if this is just what life is supposed to be like.

You've thought about getting help, but when? Finding time for therapy on top of everything else feels impossible. The waitlists in Durham are months long. And driving somewhere for an appointment? That's another thing to manage. So you keep pushing it off.

If any of this lands, you're not broken. You're just dealing with a lot. And virtual therapy can meet you where you are.

How Anxiety Therapy Actually Helps

Therapy isn't about fixing you. You're not broken. It's about understanding what's driving the anxiety and developing a different relationship with it.

Here's what I've seen working with clients from Durham Region: the anxiety usually makes sense. There are real pressures. Real responsibilities. The commute, the job, the family, the cost of living. The problem isn't that you worry. The problem is that the worry has taken over, and you've lost the ability to step back from it.

I use Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which has strong research support. But the specific approach matters less than whether we actually connect and whether our conversations help you see things differently. ACT-based therapy shows 60-80% improvement rates within 12-16 sessions (peer-reviewed research).

What we typically work on:

  • Understanding why your anxiety shows up when and how it does
  • Developing awareness without judgment (harder than it sounds)
  • Getting clear on what you actually value, not just what you "should" do
  • Building flexibility in how you respond to stress
  • Learning to be present instead of always three steps ahead

The goal isn't to eliminate anxiety. That's not realistic. The goal is to stop letting it run your life.
