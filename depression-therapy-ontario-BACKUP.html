How Therapy Helps with Depression

Depression therapy isn't about forcing positivity or pretending everything is fine. Instead, it's about developing practical tools to navigate difficult emotions while gradually reconnecting with activities, relationships, and values that give your life meaning.

The primary approach I use is Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which has strong research support for depression. Rather than trying to eliminate depressive thoughts and feelings, ACT helps you change your relationship with them so they don't control your behavior and life choices.

What This Looks Like in Practice:

Instead of Fighting Depression, You Learn to:

  • Observe Without Judgment: Notice depressive thoughts without automatically believing them as facts
  • Take Meaningful Action: Move toward what matters to you, even when motivation is low
  • Build Behavioral Activation: Gradually re-engage with activities that bring connection and purpose
  • Practice Self-Compassion: Develop a kinder internal voice and treat yourself with understanding
  • Connect with Values: Identify what truly matters to you and take small steps toward those things
  • Increase Psychological Flexibility: Respond to situations based on your values rather than your mood

This approach isn't about toxic positivity or ignoring real problems in your life. It's about developing the skills to navigate difficult periods while still engaging in activities and relationships that give your life meaning and connection.

Many clients find that as they practice these skills, their relationship with depression fundamentally changes. Instead of seeing depression as proof that something is wrong with them, they begin to understand it as one experience among many that doesn't have to define their entire life.

Frequently Asked Questions

"How do I know if what I'm experiencing is depression?"

If persistent feelings of emptiness, exhaustion, or disconnection are affecting your daily life, relationships, or ability to engage in activities you once enjoyed, these may be signs of depression worth exploring with a professional.

"Is virtual therapy really effective for depression?"

Yes, research consistently shows virtual therapy is just as effective as in-person treatment for depression. Many clients find it easier to access regular support when they don't have to manage transportation or leaving home during difficult periods.

"What if I don't have the energy to talk much?"

That's completely understandable and something I work with regularly. Sessions can be adjusted to your current capacityâ€”sometimes we'll talk more, sometimes less. There's no pressure to perform or be "a good therapy client."

"How long does it take to feel better?"

Recovery from depression is different for everyone. Some people notice small improvements within a few weeks, while others need more time to develop coping tools and create lasting change. We'll work at your pace without pressure or timelines.

"Will you keep what I share confidential?"

Absolutely. As a Registered Psychotherapist, I'm bound by strict confidentiality requirements. What we discuss in therapy stays between us, with very limited exceptions related to safety as required by law.

"What if I need support between sessions?"

You can email me between sessions with questions or to share observations about techniques we've discussed. While I can't provide therapy via email, I can offer brief clarification and support as needed.
